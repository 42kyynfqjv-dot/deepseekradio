# State legislature & election-night grounding (Wending statehouse simulator)

## PART A — State Legislature Procedure (Small-State Scale: VT/WY/DE)

### A.1 Chamber sizes & session calendar

| State | House | Senate | Total | Term | Session type |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vermont | 150 | 30 | 180 | 2-yr biennium | Annual sessions within biennium; convenes Jan (Wed after 1st Mon), adjourns ~mid-May (typically sine die early-mid May; e.g. 2024 adjourned mid-May after ~18 legislative weeks) |
| Wyoming | 60 | 31 (30 historically, now 31) | 91 | 2-yr | **Biennial hybrid**: odd years = General Session, 2nd Tue of Jan, max **40 legislative days**; even years = Budget Session, 2nd Mon of Feb, max **20 legislative days** |
| Delaware | 41 | 21 | 62 | 2-yr (General Assembly numbered, e.g. 153rd) | Annual, Jan–June; convenes 2nd Tue of Jan; **hard constitutional deadline midnight June 30** — unpassed bills die, must be refiled next year (only true "hard wall" among the three) |

### A.2 Full bill lifecycle (stage names + realistic timing)

1. **Drafting** — via Legislative Council/LSO staff at member request; 1 day–several weeks pre-session.
2. **Introduction / First Reading** — bill given number (H.xxx/S.xxx or HB/SF), read by title only, referred to committee same day or next legislative day.
3. **Committee referral** — automatic by subject-matter jurisdiction (Speaker/President Pro Tem or Rules Committee assigns).
4. **Committee hearings** — public testimony, agency witnesses; typically 1–4 hearings over 1–6 weeks for substantive bills; many bills get **zero hearings** (die without a vote).
5. **Markup / amendment in committee** — walk-through, member amendments, redraft by counsel.
6. **Committee vote / report** — reports "ought to pass," "ought to pass as amended," "without recommendation," or simply never acts (functional death). Majority-of-committee-quorum vote.
7. **Calendar placement** — bill goes to House/Senate calendar (VT: "Notice" then "Action" calendar system); Rules Committee controls floor scheduling in DE/WY.
8. **Second reading** — floor debate opens, amendments offered/voted (simple majority, voice or roll call).
9. **Third reading** — final floor vote; passage threshold = **simple majority of those present & voting** (quorum met) in all three states for ordinary bills.
10. **Crossover deadline** — bill must pass its chamber of origin and be transmitted to the other chamber by a fixed mid-session date or it is generally barred from further action that year (committee can seek "extension of privilege" from Rules to survive). VT 2024 crossover: **March 15** (Senate bills to House committee by ~Mar 22, "Crossover" proper). WY: crossover deadlines are tight given the whole session is only 20–40 days (bills must pass origin chamber ~day 13 of a 20-day budget session). DE: no formal mid-session crossover deadline as strict as VT/WY — the operative deadline is the June 30 sine die wall itself.
11. **Second chamber repeats steps 2–9** (introduction as a "received" bill, referral, hearings, markup, floor vote).
12. **Conference committee** — convened only if chambers pass differing versions and neither concurs; typically 3 members per chamber (chairs + ranking); resolves in 1–10 days; report is subject to up-or-down vote, no further amendment.
13. **Enrollment** — clerk prepares final engrossed/enrolled bill.
14. **Governor action** — sign (becomes Act/law), allow to become law without signature (VT/DE allow this after set day count), or veto.
    - VT: governor has 5 days (session) / 10 days (post-adjournment) to act; unsigned bill becomes law if legislature in session, dies if adjourned ("pocket veto" only post sine die).
    - WY: governor has 3 days (in session) / 15 days (after adjournment).
    - DE: governor has 10 days.
15. **Veto override** — **two-thirds vote of members elected (not just present) in each chamber** in all three states (VT Const. Ch.II §11; WY Const. Art.4 §8; DE Const. Art.3 §18). Override sessions rare — small-state governors of same party as legislative majority veto <5 bills/year typically; override attempts succeed <20% of the time nationally.

Sources: [Vermont Legislative Process PDF](https://legislature.vermont.gov/assets/All-House-Documents/Vermont-Legislative-Process.pdf), [VTDigger crossover explainer](https://vtdigger.org/2024/01/22/how-to-read-the-legislative-calendars/), [Vermont Public — crossover deadline coverage](https://www.vermontpublic.org/local-news/2024-03-22/capitol-recap-which-climate-and-environment-bills-made-the-crossover-deadline), [NCSL Veto Overrides and Supermajorities](https://www.ncsl.org/center-for-legislative-strengthening/veto-overrides-and-supermajorities), [Ballotpedia Veto overrides in state legislatures](https://ballotpedia.org/Veto_overrides_in_state_legislatures)

### A.4 Leadership roles (uniform across states)

- **Speaker of the House** — presiding officer, appoints committee chairs/members (VT/WY), refers bills, controls House floor calendar.
- **President / President Pro Tempore of the Senate** — in states with a Lt. Governor as nominal Senate president (DE), the Pro Tem runs day-to-day; VT/WY Senates elect their own Pro Tem as working presiding officer.
- **Majority Leader / Minority Leader** — each chamber, each party; manage floor strategy, scheduling of caucus priorities.
- **Whips** (Majority/Minority) — count votes, coordinate members; in small chambers (VT House minority sometimes <50 members) whip role is often informal/combined with leader.
- **Committee Chairs/Vice-Chairs** — control committee agenda, hearing scheduling, "hostile" bill deaths by simply never scheduling a hearing.

### A.5 Quorum rules

- All three: **majority of elected members of a chamber** constitutes quorum for ordinary business — i.e., VT House 76/150, VT Senate 16/30; WY House 31/60, WY Senate 16/31; DE House 21/41, DE Senate 11/21.
- Vermont has an elevated special quorum (2/3) only for raising a state tax.
- Below-quorum minority can compel attendance of absent members ("call of the house") under chamber rules.

Sources: [Wyoming Constitution via Justia](https://law.justia.com/constitution/wyoming/), [Vermont Legislative Process PDF](https://legislature.vermont.gov/assets/Clerk-of-the-House-Documents/Overview-and-Training-Docs/Overview-of-Vermonts-Legislative-Process.pdf)

### A.6 Vote types

- **Voice vote** — default for uncontested/procedural motions and most committee "ought to pass" reports; ~60–75% of all floor actions in small chambers.
- **Roll call / recorded vote** — required for final passage of bills of consequence, triggered automatically for veto overrides, constitutional amendments, tax bills, or on demand of a small fixed number of members (VT: 6 members can demand roll call in House; WY and DE have similar low member-count triggers, typically 1/5 of members present).
- **Division vote** (standing count, no names recorded) — intermediate step used to test a voice vote result before going to a full roll call.
- Roll-call votes on final passage run roughly **20–35%** of all bills that reach a floor vote in small chambers; the rest pass on voice vote. (This range subsumes the "voice ~65–75%" framing below — treat 65–75% voice / 25–35% roll call as the reconciled split.)

### A.7 Bill volume, enactment rate, committee death rate

- National 2023–24 biennium: **~246,405 bills introduced** across all 50 states; overall state enactment rate **≈25.8%** of introduced bills become law (vs. ~5.5% in US Congress). [MultiState insider report](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2024/12/11/state-lawmakers-introduce-over-a-quarter-million-bills-each-season), [Quorum state legislative trends](https://www.quorum.us/data-driven-insights/state-legislatures-versus-congress-which-is-more-productive/)
- Small full-time/hybrid states scale down proportionally:
  - **Vermont**: typically **600–800 bills introduced** per biennium (House + Senate combined), of which roughly **150–220 Acts** are passed into law per biennium (~22–28% enactment rate), consistent with national average.
  - **Wyoming**: General session (odd yr, 40 days) sees **~350–450 bills** introduced; Budget session (even yr, 20 days) is much lighter, **~150–250 bills**, because non-budget bills need a **2/3 supermajority vote just to be introduced** (a unique WY filter that suppresses volume and raises effective enactment % of what's introduced, ~40–55% in budget years since most surviving intro bills already had supermajority support).
  - **Delaware**: **~700–900 bills** introduced per 2-year General Assembly, with **~350–450 enacted** (≈45–50% — DE's rate runs higher than the national average partly because of its short, all-or-nothing June 30 wall concentrating leadership-blessed bills late session).
- **Committee death rate**: across small states, roughly **55–70% of introduced bills never get out of committee** (no hearing, or hearing but no vote/report) — this is the single largest bill-mortality stage, far exceeding floor-stage defeats (floor-vote failure rate on bills that do reach the floor is low, typically <5%, since leadership generally only calendars bills they expect to pass).
- Bill-sponsorship caps suppress volume in Wyoming specifically: **max 7 bills per legislator** in a general session (fewer effectively in budget session due to the 2/3 introduction hurdle). [Wyoming Senate Rules](https://www.wyoleg.gov/docs/SenateRules.pdf)

Sources: [Vermont Bills Introduced 2024](https://legislature.vermont.gov/bill/introduced/2024), [Vermont Bills Passed 2024](https://legislature.vermont.gov/bill/passed/2024), [LegiScan VT](https://legiscan.com/VT), [LegiScan WY](https://legiscan.com/WY), [Delaware LegiScan](https://legiscan.com/DE), [MultiState quarter-million bills report](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2024/12/11/state-lawmakers-introduce-over-a-quarter-million-bills-each-season)

## PART B — Election-Night Returns Mechanics

### B.1 Poll closing cadence (drives when returns *can* start)

Polls close state-by-state, staggered 6:00pm–1:00am ET. Key clusters: **7:00pm ET** (IN*, KY-most, GA, SC, VA, VT, NH-most) → **7:30pm ET** (NC, OH, WV) → **8:00pm ET** (AL, CT, DE, FL-most, IL, ME, MD, MA, MI, MS, MO, NJ, OK, PA, RI, TN, TX-most, DC) → **8:30pm ET** (AR) → **9:00pm ET** (AZ, CO, KS, LA, MN, NE, NM, NY, ND, SD, WI, WY) → **10:00pm ET** (IA, MT, NV, UT) → **11:00pm ET** (CA, ID-most, OR-most, WA) → **1:00am ET** (AK, HI). Some states split zones (IN, KY, FL, TX, MI, NE, ND, ID, OR).

Sources: [270toWin](https://www.270towin.com/poll-closing-times/), [Ballotpedia 2026](https://ballotpedia.org/State_Poll_Opening_and_Closing_Times_(2026))

### B.2 First-returns timing

- First raw numbers typically post 5–20 minutes after local poll close — these are pre-processed early/mail ballots states permit pre-counting (e.g., FL, GA, NC, AZ start tabulating early/mail votes before Election Day; PA/WI legally forbid pre-processing until Election Day morning, so their first dump is delayed and mail-heavy).
- Pennsylvania: mail ballots cannot even begin being *opened* until 7:00am Election Day, so PA reliably lags peer states by hours. [NBC](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/2024-election/red-blue-mirage-election-night-vote-counts-make-hard-tell-will-win-rcna175475)
- North Carolina in 2024 targeted ~90% of votes reported by midnight ET. [CBC](https://www.cbc.ca/news/world/swing-state-polls-vote-counting-1.7372724)
- Arizona: no results released until either all precincts report or 1 hour post-close, whichever first — historically ~10:00pm ET first drop. [LiveNOW/FOX](https://www.livenowfox.com/news/election-results-vote-count-timeline-swing-states-2024)
- Large urban counties with heavy mail volume (Maricopa AZ, Clark NV, Philadelphia/Allegheny PA, Milwaukee/Dane WI, Fulton/DeKalb GA) are chronically the LAST to finish counting — Milwaukee's central count characteristically reports late night/early morning. [CBC](https://www.cbc.ca/news/world/swing-state-polls-vote-counting-1.7372724)

### B.3 % precincts reporting cadence (generic shape to model)

Reporting is NOT linear — it's a sigmoid/step function dominated by county size:
- **T+0 to T+1hr**: 0–15% in — small/rural precincts (hand-count or simple tabulators, few ballots) report first; also any pre-processed early/mail batches drop as a lump near poll close.
- **T+1 to T+3hr**: 40–75% in — mid-size counties and suburbs finish; this is the volatile "mirage" window.
- **T+3 to T+5hr**: 85–97% in — most precincts done; large urban/mail-heavy counties still trickling.
- **T+5hr+ (post-midnight to next day)**: final 3–15% — big-county mail/provisional/absentee tail, often taking 1–4 days to fully resolve (Nevada historically 4+ days; California regularly counts for 2–3 weeks due to mail-ballot cure periods).
- Rule of thumb for the generator: **rural/small precincts report fast and skew whichever party dominates in-person Election-Day turnout that cycle; large urban/mail-heavy precincts report slow and skew the opposite direction.**

### B.4 Urban vs. rural reporting order — mechanism

- Rural precincts: fewer ballots, fewer machines needed, closer polling-place-to-tabulation logistics, less litigation/observer friction → report FAST.
- Urban/mail-heavy counties: hundreds of thousands of mail ballots requiring signature verification, extraction, flattening, scanning in batches at a handful of central-count facilities → report SLOW and in large discrete "dumps" (not smooth trickle) — a single county dump can swing a statewide margin by multiple points in seconds.
- States that pre-process mail ballots before Election Day (FL, GA, NC, AZ, CO, ~most West-mail states) release these early → early lead often favors the party with the *early/mail* edge that cycle (Dem-leaning in 2020/2022, more mixed 2024 as GOP increased early voting). States barring pre-processing (PA, WI, MI-partial) show the reverse sequencing: Election-Day in-person counted first (recent history GOP-leaning), mail counted last (recent history Dem-leaning).

### B.5 Red mirage / blue shift — definition & magnitude

- **Red mirage**: early lead for the party whose voters disproportionately vote in-person on Election Day (recently Republicans) because same-day votes get counted/reported first in states like PA/WI/MI.
- **Blue shift** (or blue mirage → red shift is the mirror case in pre-processing states): late-counted mail/absentee/provisional ballots skew the other way, narrowing or flipping the apparent margin over hours-to-days.
- 2020 Pennsylvania: Trump led by ~600–700K votes on election night; final certified margin flipped to Biden +80,555 — a **swing of roughly 700K+ raw votes / ~9–10 points** as mail ballots were tabulated over the following days. Use this as the outer bound for a "big shift" scenario.
- 2024: shift was smaller/muted in most swing states because more Republicans voted early/by mail and some states (Michigan) allowed pre-processing — model a REDUCED shift magnitude (roughly half of 2020's) as the "new normal," but keep the PA/WI-style full mirage available for narrative variety. [NBC](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/2024-election/red-blue-mirage-election-night-vote-counts-make-hard-tell-will-win-rcna175475), [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2024/11/05/trump-harris-2024-election-absentee-voting-red-mirage)
- North Carolina 2024: blue-mirage-then-red pattern (mail counted first → early Dem lean → Election Day in-person flips it Republican → final small Dem-leaning mail trickle). [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/saradorn/2024/11/04/blue-mirage-and-red-mirage-explained-why-tomorrows-first-swing-state-vote-counts-may-be-misleading/)

### B.6 How AP / networks CALL races

- AP decision desk: ~60 analysts/race-callers/editors; a network of ~4,000 stringers phone in raw vote-count totals for cross-verification against official feeds. [PBS](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/how-the-associated-press-projects-winners-on-election-night)
- Call trigger is NOT "100% counted" — AP calls **as soon as the trailing candidate has a mathematical/statistical zero chance to catch up**, combining: (a) raw running vote count and margin, (b) which precincts/counties are still outstanding and their known partisan lean from historical results ("key precincts"/bellwethers), (c) AP VoteCast (large-sample validated post-vote survey, tens of thousands of respondents, replacing exit polls since 2018) for demographic cross-checks, (d) ballot-type composition (mail vs. in-person vs. provisional) to model how remaining vote will break. [Nieman Reports](https://niemanreports.org/how-the-ap-calls-elections/), [Bipartisan Policy Center](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/explainer/how-media-outlets-call-races-from-unofficial-election-results/)
- Networks (NBC/CBS/ABC/Fox/CNN) run **independent decision desks** using similar inputs plus internal models; calls between outlets can differ in TIMING by minutes to hours even on identical underlying data, occasionally causing on-air "too early to call" friction — a realistic broadcast beat.
- **Landslide/uncompetitive races**: often called within seconds to minutes of poll close using pre-election modeling + tiny initial sample, when historical partisan lean makes the outcome statistically certain regardless of count order.
- **Close races** (single digits, especially <3pts): calls withheld for hours to days; a race can go from "too early to call" to "called" without any new dramatic vote dump — just accumulated certainty as expected outstanding vote shrinks.
- AP explicitly will NOT call a race it believes will trigger an automatic/mandatory recount under state law, even with a clear numerical leader, until that threshold math is resolved.

### B.7 Precinct / county counts for scale (small-state modeling)

- US total: roughly **170,000–200,000 precincts** nationally (EAC 2004 figure: 174,252; more recent estimates cluster near 175K–190K depending on consolidation), across **3,143 counties/county-equivalents**. [EAC/Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electoral_precinct)
- Structural quirk: **Vermont** organizes by town/city (~237 towns), not counties; **Delaware** by legislative-district-coded precincts across only **3 counties** (New Castle, Kent, Sussex); **Wyoming** is county-based across **23 counties** with typically single-digit-to-low-double-digit precincts per rural county.
- Small states typically have on the order of **150–400 precincts statewide**; mid states **1,000–3,000**; large states (CA, TX, NY, FL, PA) **8,000–25,000+**.

### B.8 Automatic recount thresholds (selected, verify per-cycle — states change law often)

| State | Automatic recount trigger |
|---|---|
| Pennsylvania | ≤0.5% margin (statewide race) |
| Minnesota | ≤0.5% margin (or ≤0.25% for some local races) |
| Arizona | ≤0.5% margin (raised from 0.1% in 2022) |
| Florida | ≤0.5% margin (machine recount); ≤0.25% triggers manual recount of just that margin band |
| Ohio | ≤0.5% margin |

### B.10 Realistic swing sizes between elections

- Presidential-to-presidential national popular vote swing: typically **2–6 points** cycle over cycle (e.g., 2020→2024 swung ~+2–3pts toward Trump nationally; 2016→2020 ~+2–3pts toward Biden).
- Midterm swings against the president's party ("wave" elections): historically **5–9 points** in generic ballot / average House margin (2018 blue wave: D+8.6 popular vote; 2022 muted red wave: ~R+2.8).
- Within-cycle election-night mirage swing (not a real electorate shift, just count-order artifact): can be **5–15 points** in a single county's reporting margin as it moves from 20%→100% counted; statewide artifact swings of **1–4 points** are common in PA/WI/MI/AZ-style states; PA 2020 saw an especially extreme ~9–10pt / 700K-vote statewide artifact swing — treat as upper-bound outlier, not median case.

### B.11 Broadcast-language cadence mapping

| Time bucket relative to poll close | Broadcast posture |
|---|---|
| Poll close to +30min | "Polls just closed in X — too early to call," early exit-poll/VoteCast-informed color commentary, uncompetitive races called instantly |
| +30min to +2hr | First real returns on screen, "X% of precincts reporting," anchors narrate early lean without calling close races, key-county watch list introduced |
| +2hr to +4hr | Bulk of precincts in; competitive races start getting called as trailing-candidate path narrows; "AP has called X for Y"; graphics show needle/model shifting |
| +4hr to +8hr (post-midnight) | Remaining calls trickle in as big urban/mail counties finish; anchors explicitly flag "we are still waiting on [county]" for uncalled races; overnight handoff if unresolved |
| Day +1 to Day +4 | Only for genuinely close races: continued counting narrative, recount-threshold discussion, concession watch |
| Concession timing | Historically same night or within 24hrs is the norm (near-universal 1896–2020 for clear losers); genuinely close races can run days-to-weeks (Gore 2000: 36 days; some 2018/2022 House races took 1–3 weeks); refusal-to-concede is a rare deviation script, not baseline |

Sources: [PBS on AP calls](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/how-the-associated-press-projects-winners-on-election-night), [Statista days-to-concession](https://www.statista.com/statistics/1186099/days-until-concession-us-elections/), [CBS concession tradition](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/an-election-night-tradition-the-concession-speech/)
