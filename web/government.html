The State of Wending

Wending is the fifty-first state, admitted because a clerk filed the founding boundary form in the ADMITTED drawer instead of the PENDING one, and nobody will fix it — fixing it needs a form that is itself pending in the same committee. Legal status, for decades: provisional, pending review. The review is now older than the reviewers. And because a pending state still has to be governed, Wending was issued the complete machinery of a real one — provisionally, and therefore permanently.

That machinery meets at the Half-Dome, the statehouse whose dome went pending mid-construction, so it sits open to the sky and tarped in winter. When the rotunda leaks they call it the Sieve. Eleven fixtures work; Fixture 12 flickers under a maintenance ticket older than most of the staff. Inside, Governor Marty Bouchard — weary, well-meaning, third-pair defenseman energy — presides over enormous business about nothing, while Clerk Gord Pelletier keeps the Book at Window 4 until 4:30 and could fix the founding error any time he liked, if it didn't mean rushing.

The Ballot

Seven parties. Sixty seats. One goose.

Wending has no left and no right. The one organizing axis is the Zipper Question — when two lanes become one, do you merge early (courteous, cautious, at the first sign) or late (efficient, zipper-at-the-cone, some say smug)? The polite "sorry, go ahead" reflex is the state's default temperament; the Vanguard are the Late-Merge radicals the rest of the field organizes against. Every whip count is read through the Zipper. Everything else, the parties trade vote by vote.
🪿 The Goose Party
THE HEADLINER · OBSESSION: THE GOOSE · OFF THE ZIPPER AXIS ENTIRELY

Elect the actual pharmacy-lot goose as Governor of Wending. That is the whole platform. There is no second plank.

The goose cannot consent, so the Party has all of its opinions for it — inventing its positions, issuing statements on its behalf, and holding rallies the goose does not attend. When the goose wanders through by coincidence, the Party calls it a landslide of momentum. In-house it is "the Candidate" (naming the real bird is, itself, in committee). In a hung House its handful of seats can decide any bill — sold only for goose-related considerations: repave the lot, a resolution recognizing the goose's tenure. It never actually governs. It has no hands, so it cannot take the oath on the roundabout plans, and the Committee on Rules refers the question to the Committee on Merging, where it stays. Then it runs again next cycle.

The Provisional Party
OBSESSION · NEVER FINISHING

Full statehood means final paperwork means it's over. Keep us provisional, forever.

Everyone runs as an "Acting Candidate." Headquarters is a U-Haul that has idled in one spot since 1987 under a laminated sign reading "TEMPORARY, DO NOT MOVE." Natural ally of anyone blocking a vote; the Compliance Office's favorite target.

The Roundabout Party
OBSESSION · THE ROUNDABOUT

Every problem, like traffic, is best handled by going around it repeatedly until someone finds an exit.

Meetings are held while physically circling Mile Zero — a motion isn't official until it completes a full lap — which leaves the Party the perpetually-hung swing bloc every coalition courts and none can pin down. "Roughly two weeks from a firm decision," always.

The Zipper Merge Vanguard
OBSESSION · THE MERGE · ANCHORS THE LATE POLE

Zipper at the cone, at the final inch. Pity the coward who merges early.

Every statement cuts off mid-sentence, refusing on principle to yield the floor until the very last possible wor—

The Office of Interparty Compliance
OBSESSION · EVERYONE ELSE'S PAPERWORK

Doesn't govern. Issues Notices of Deficiency against every party that does — the system's built-in antagonist, present in every storyline.

Fields no candidates. Audits the goose's filing, rejects the Provisional permit, flags the Grudge Society's fruit-basket receipts. Once rejected its own registration, for being submitted in blue ink.

The Committee to Formally Address What Barbara Said in 1987
OBSESSION · ONE COMMENT AT A BRIDAL SHOWER

A fully-funded legislative bloc organized around a single remark, made once, at a bridal shower, in 1987.

Barbara has never been informed and, at last check, sits on the Committee — she thinks it's about drainage. The Grudge Preservation Society finds them dangerously soft, on the grounds that they might one day want closure.

The Grudge Preservation Society
OBSESSION · KEEPING FEUDS ALIVE

Reconciliation is surrender. A feud, properly maintained, is a civic asset.

When two families near a truce, the Society anonymously sends one of them a fruit basket — a known act of aggression in these parts — to reset the grudge to zero. Considers the Barbara Committee alarmingly close to healing.

Election Night, From the Booth

Same crew as the hockey. Same vocabulary. Bucky Merle & Sal Tarantella call the returns.

● LIVE RETURNS · THE BOOTH CALLS THE HALF-DOME

Election Night is called exactly like a game. Precincts are periods. A race too close to call goes to a recount — which is overtime — and re-narrates the same result slower, with more ceremony, until the margin is official. A weather-delayed poll is a rain-out, called straight. Governor Bouchard's approval rides the broadcast like a streak: a filled pothole is a goal, a botched filing a penalty.

PRECINCTS = PERIODS RECOUNT = OVERTIME WEATHER DELAY = RAIN-OUT PROVISIONAL BALLOTS = UNDER REVIEW APPROVAL = THE STREAK

BUCKY: Pharmacy-Lot Precinct reporting — it's a seven-vote margin with the overflow box still out. Seven-vote game here late.

SAL: And Bucky, she's leading among voters who parallel-park on the first try — a demographic that has never been wrong in an odd year.

BUCKY: Overflow box is in — and we are tied. We're going to a recount.

SAL: Overtime. You love to see it. Fundamentals win recounts, Bucky — positioning, hydration, and getting there first at the four-way stop.

Recurring Officials

Canon in office, swappable in person. Every one of them could play third-pair defense.

Governor Marty Bouchard

GOVERNOR · THE OFFICIAL RESIDENCE (HALF-DUPLEX)

Weary, well-meaning, sounds like he could kill a penalty on the third pair. Takes the oath with one hand on the laminated roundabout master plans — sworn upon precisely because they will never be finished, making the oath technically eternal. His approval lives and dies on potholes and on things he did not do and cannot fix. Feuds with his duplex neighbor over the shared driveway, which is, of course, a merge question.

Gord Pelletier

CLERK OF THE ROLL · CLERK OF THE DRAWER · 41 YEARS

Keeper of the Book, and the human embodiment of the founding error. He could fix it — a signature filed in the wrong ink — but that would mean rushing, and Gord does not rush. The single most powerful person in the state. He will be at Window 4 until 4:30.

Bert Demers

POTHOLE COMMISSIONER · UP FOR REELECTION

Campaigns on the potholes personally. He has named them. He has feelings about each and considers "just filling them" a betrayal — his challenger wants them all filled, which Bert calls "erasure." Perennially failing, humiliated daily by the goose's well-run lot across the roundabout.

Toivo Ostberg

FOREMAN · THE ROUNDABOUT (MILE ZERO)

Has said "we're about two weeks out" for nine years and means it every time — fresh conviction, no memory of the last time. His grievance is with the public, who keep driving on it while it's nearly done.
